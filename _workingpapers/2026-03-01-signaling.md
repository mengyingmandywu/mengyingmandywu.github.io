excerpt: 'Abstract: 
China’s nationwide environmental inspections were designed to strengthen local environmental enforcement through high-profile central scrutiny. This paper argues that when inspections are not backed by consistent punishment or durable follow-up, they instead operate as soft signals: local governments and regulated firms learn that compliance is temporary and reduce effort in subsequent rounds. I formalize this mechanism in a repeated central–local signaling game with learning, where observed sanctions, legal actions, and post-inspection pollution rebound update beliefs about the Center’s effective enforcement “toughness.” A simple calibration—disciplined by observed enforcement intensity and the persistence of pollution changes—yields quantitative benchmarks for the punishment needed to sustain compliance. Empirically, I construct a city-week panel of ambient air pollution from China’s national monitoring network (validated against satellite-based measures) and match outcomes to the staggered timing of the original inspection rounds (2015–2017) and the “lookback” round (2018). I estimate dynamic impacts using a stacked event-study design indexed in weeks relative to the on-site inspection window, with city and calendar-week fixed effects. To test the learning mechanism, I relate lookback responses to first-round enforcement and follow-up—rectification orders, shutdowns, filed cases, fines, and cadre discipline—and examine whether strategic reorganization in later rounds restores credibility and persistence. I also link enforcement intensity to local officials’ career outcomes and formal environmental law actions.'
